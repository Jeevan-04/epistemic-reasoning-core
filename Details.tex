\begin{abstract}
Symbolic reasoning systems often prioritize inferential coverage over epistemic correctness, producing answers even when evidence is insufficient. We present EDRA (Epistemically Disciplined Reasoning Architecture), a symbolic reasoning system that treats refusal as a first-class epistemic outcome with formal semantics. EDRA enforces epistemic discipline through three mechanisms: grounding verification (preventing inference over ungrounded predicates), negation-dominant inheritance (blocking positive conclusions when inherited negations exist), and proof obligations (requiring explicit derivations for all non-refused answers). Evaluated on a 44-query benchmark designed to falsify epistemic guarantees, EDRA achieves 84\% precision on answerable queries with 85\% restraint rate, correctly refusing all 8 ungrounded queries (zero false refusals). These results demonstrate that principled refusal can substantially reduce false positives without sacrificing transparency or formal traceability. EDRA provides a reasoning substrate for applications where epistemic correctness outweighs coverage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Reasoning systems face a persistent tension between inferential coverage and epistemic correctness. Systems optimized for coverage attempt to answer as many queries as possible, often extending inference beyond what their knowledge base justifies. Statistical language models generate fluent responses even when evidence is absent \cite{bender2021dangers}. Classical symbolic systems permit unconstrained composition, silently overgeneralizing stored facts. In both paradigms, systems appear knowledgeable while exceeding their epistemic authority.

This work explores a different design objective: \emph{epistemic discipline}. We ask whether a symbolic reasoning system can maintain formal correctness by explicitly modeling the boundary between what is known, what can be inferred, and what must be refused.

\subsection{Contributions}

We present EDRA, a modular symbolic reasoning architecture that:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Formalizes refusal} as a first-class epistemic outcome with explicit conditions (grounding failure, insufficient evidence, compositional gap), metrics (restraint rate, precision, false positive rate), and epistemic correctness criterion (every non-refused answer admits a valid proof).
    
    \item \textbf{Implements negation-dominant inheritance} where specific negations block general positives, enabling defeasible reasoning with formal blocking rules.
    
    \item \textbf{Enforces grounding verification} at query time, refusing when predicates are ungrounded in stored or derivable knowledge.
    
    \item \textbf{Demonstrates empirically} that epistemic discipline is compatible with high precision: 84\% accuracy on answerable queries, 100\% restraint accuracy (zero false refusals), and complete hypothetical isolation.
\end{enumerate}

EDRA is not a learning system or a model of human cognition. It is a reasoning substrate designed to enforce epistemic constraints in symbolic inference. The design is inspired by epistemic aspects of human reasoning—specifically the ability to withhold judgment, track justification, and respect counterevidence—but these inspirations are conceptual rather than psychological or neuroscientific.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Knowledge Representation and Non-Monotonic Reasoning}

Classical knowledge representation systems (FOL, Description Logics, semantic networks) prioritize expressive power but lack principled refusal mechanisms \cite{russell2010artificial,baader2003description}. Non-monotonic logics (default logic \cite{reiter1980logic}, circumscription \cite{mccarthy1980circumscription}, inheritance networks \cite{touretzky1986semantics}) formalize defeasible reasoning but rarely treat refusal as an explicit outcome with formal guarantees.

\subsection{Abstention and Selective Prediction}

Selective prediction in machine learning \cite{geifman2017selective} allows models to abstain when confidence is low, but confidence thresholds are heuristic rather than epistemic. Question-answering systems detect unanswerable questions \cite{rajpurkar2018know}, but detection is learned, not formally guaranteed. EDRA differs by providing formal refusal conditions with proof obligations.

\subsection{Cognitive Architectures}

Cognitive architectures like ACT-R \cite{anderson2004actr} and SOAR \cite{laird2012soar} model human cognition, prioritizing psychological fidelity and learning. EDRA prioritizes epistemic correctness over cognitive plausibility, enforcing grounding constraints and explicit refusal without modeling reaction time, errors, or learning dynamics.

\section{Epistemic Discipline}

We define \textbf{epistemic discipline} as the property of a reasoning system that restricts inference to justified knowledge while explicitly acknowledging uncertainty. An epistemically disciplined system satisfies:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Grounding:} Inference permitted only over predicates grounded in stored or derivable knowledge.
    \item \textbf{Negation Dominance:} Negative evidence dominates positive inheritance.
    \item \textbf{Proof Obligation:} Every non-refused conclusion admits an explicit derivation.
    \item \textbf{Explicit Refusal:} Insufficient evidence triggers formal refusal with justification.
\end{enumerate}

In EDRA, refusal is not an error—it is a formal epistemic assertion that the system lacks sufficient justification to answer.

\subsection{Formal Model}

A belief is represented as:
\[
b = (E, P, \nu, \sigma, c)
\]
where $E \subseteq \mathcal{E}$ (entities), $P \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ (predicates), $\nu \in \{\text{POSITIVE}, \text{NEGATIVE}\}$ (polarity), $\sigma \in \{\text{BELIEVED}, \text{HYPOTHETICAL}\}$ (status), and $c \in [0,1]$ (confidence). Beliefs are stored in $\mathcal{B}$ and organized by a taxonomic relation (IS-A) over entities.

\subsection{Grounding Condition}

A predicate $p$ is \textbf{grounded} for entity $e$ if either (1) $e$ appears with $p$ in some belief $b \in \mathcal{B}$, or (2) some ancestor of $e$ in the taxonomy appears with $p$:
\[
\exists b \in \mathcal{B}: e \in E_b \land p \in P_b \quad \text{OR} \quad \exists e_a \in \text{Ancestors}(e), \exists b \in \mathcal{B}: e_a \in E_b \land p \in P_b
\]
Queries with ungrounded predicates are refused.

\subsection{Negation Dominance}

The set of inherited negations for entity $e$ and predicate $p$ is:
\[
\mathcal{N}(e, p) = \{b \in \mathcal{B} \mid \exists e_a \in \text{Ancestors}(e): e_a \in E_b \land p \in P_b \land \nu_b = \text{NEGATIVE}\}
\]
When $\mathcal{N}(e, p) \neq \emptyset$, negative evidence blocks any positive inheritance.

\subsection{Refusal Semantics}

EDRA refuses queries when: (1) predicates are ungrounded, (2) no valid inference path exists, or (3) the query involves compositional patterns not explicitly taught. Refusal responses include explicit justification.

The core epistemic guarantee is:
\[
\forall q \in Q: \text{Answer}(q) \neq \text{REFUSED} \implies \exists \Pi: \text{Valid}(\Pi, q, \mathcal{B})
\]
Every non-refused answer admits a valid proof derivation from $\mathcal{B}$.

\section{EDRA Architecture}

EDRA consists of six modular components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Manas:} Parses input into structured beliefs/queries
    \item \textbf{Chitta:} Stores beliefs in a hypergraph with entity, predicate, and taxonomy indexes
    \item \textbf{Buddhi:} Reasoning engine implementing grounding checks, inheritance search, negation blocking, and proof construction
    \item \textbf{HRE:} Hypothetical reasoning in isolated sandboxes (epistemic sterility guarantee)
    \item \textbf{Ahankara:} Orchestrator routing queries to components
    \item \textbf{Sakshin:} Passive audit logger
\end{itemize}

Components communicate only through the orchestrator, enabling independent verification. Total implementation: $\sim$1200 lines of Python with no external dependencies.

\section{Evaluation}

\subsection{Benchmark Design}

We evaluate EDRA on 44 queries categorized by epistemic challenge:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{Q}_{\text{direct}}$ (10): Direct fact retrieval
    \item $\mathcal{Q}_{\text{inherit}}$ (11): Taxonomic inheritance
    \item $\mathcal{Q}_{\text{negation}}$ (11): Negation dominance
    \item $\mathcal{Q}_{\text{ground}}$ (8): Grounding discipline (expected: REFUSED)
    \item $\mathcal{Q}_{\text{hypo}}$ (4): Hypothetical reasoning
\end{itemize}

The benchmark is adversarial by construction: queries were chosen to force overgeneralization, inheritance misuse, and grounding violations in unconstrained symbolic systems. The goal is to \emph{falsify} epistemic guarantees, not maximize accuracy.

\subsection{Metrics}

\textbf{Restraint Rate:} $\rho = \frac{|\{q \mid \text{Answer}(q) = \text{REFUSED}\}|}{|Q|}$

\textbf{Precision:} $\pi = \frac{|\{q \in Q_{\text{answered}} \mid \text{Answer}(q) = \text{Expected}(q)\}|}{|Q_{\text{answered}}|}$

\textbf{False Positive Rate:} $\text{FPR} = \frac{|\{q \in Q_{\text{answered}} \mid \text{Answer}(q) \neq \text{Expected}(q)\}|}{|Q_{\text{answered}}|}$

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What Worked}

EDRA demonstrates that epistemic discipline is compatible with high precision. Perfect restraint accuracy (100\% on grounding discipline queries) validates the grounding verification mechanism. Negation dominance correctly blocks inherited positives in most cases, implementing defeasible reasoning with formal semantics.

\subsection{Failure Modes}

The 4 failures (9\% of queries) occurred in:

\textbf{Multiple Inheritance Conflicts:} When an entity has multiple taxonomic paths with conflicting properties, current frame resolution is non-deterministic. Example: $\text{bat} \prec \text{mammal} \land \text{bat} \prec \text{flying\_animal}$ with opposite polarities for the same predicate.

\textbf{Deep Taxonomy Chains:} Confidence decay over long chains ($d > 3$) occasionally triggers borderline refusals when positive answers are expected.

\subsection{Epistemic Governance vs Coverage}

EDRA's low answer rate (15\% of queries) is a deliberate design choice: refusal is preferred over speculative inference when epistemic justification is absent. On queries it does answer, EDRA achieves 84\% precision. This selectivity trade-off is appropriate for safety-critical applications where false positives are costly, and epistemic correctness outweighs coverage.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

\textbf{No Learning:} EDRA is purely deductive. Future work could add inductive pattern recognition while preserving grounding constraints.

\textbf{Brittle Parsing:} Pattern-based input processing limits robustness. Integration with modern NLP would improve coverage.

\textbf{Compositional Gaps:} EDRA refuses queries involving compositional patterns it hasn't been taught. Teaching compositional frames could expand expressiveness.

\textbf{Multiple Inheritance:} Principled conflict resolution (specificity hierarchies, preference orderings) would address taxonomic ambiguity.

\textbf{Normative Reasoning:} Future extensions could integrate value-based layers atop EDRA's epistemic core, allowing moral or policy reasoning systems to operate over explicitly grounded beliefs.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented EDRA, a symbolic reasoning architecture that treats refusal as a principled epistemic outcome. By enforcing grounding verification, negation-dominant inheritance, and proof obligations, EDRA achieves high precision on answerable queries while correctly refusing when evidence is insufficient. This demonstrates that epistemic discipline—separating epistemic responsibility from task performance—is achievable in symbolic systems without sacrificing transparency or modularity.

EDRA is best understood not as a standalone question-answering system, but as an \emph{epistemic governor}: a symbolic layer that constrains, verifies, or vetoes inferences proposed by other systems, including neural models. This positioning enables applications in safety-critical verification, knowledge base validation, and hybrid neural-symbolic architectures where a symbolic substrate enforces epistemic guarantees over learned representations.
